Over a decade ago, Hito Steryl posed the question of How Not to Be Seen, and the answer may be to become Israel on Google Earth. In 1997, the Kyl-Bingaman Amendment (KBA) was signed into United States law, prohibiting the US from collecting and disseminating high resolution satellite imagery of Israel—the only country privileged to such a sweeping censorship law. As a result, for more than 20 years, Israel (and by implicit extension, the occupied Palestinian territories and the occupied Golan Heights) have only existed in blurry pixels on Google Earth, obscuring the expansion of Israeli settlements in the West Bank as well as the extent of destruction to Palestinian homes. In 2020, the KBA was amended to once again allow high resolution satellite images of Israel. However, upon closer inspection, large swaths of the country fall through the gaps: while most of its populated areas are now accessible on Google Earth, there are still neighbourhoods and towns that are incredibly blurry—a different kind of pixelation, one that turns the landscape into painterly images.
